For millions of people, the barrier to medication adherence isn't forgetting the dose ‚Äî it's being in the wrong place when the reminder fires. Standard time-based reminders solve the right problem with the wrong tool.

The Problem with Time-Only Reminders

Here's a scenario most medication users recognize:

üì± The Missed Reminder Scenario

Your 7 PM medication reminder fires while you're still at the office, commuting, or picking up the kids. You mentally note "I'll take it when I get home." You get home at 8:30 PM, put down your bag, and go straight into dinner preparation. By 10 PM, you realize you never took it.

The reminder did technically fire. But it fired in a context where you couldn't act on it ‚Äî and by the time you were in the right context, the reminder had long been dismissed.

Why Context Matters for Memory

Memory researchers have documented the "context-dependency of memory" for decades: we remember things better in the context where we encoded them. A reminder that fires when you're at your desk encodes the "take medication" intention in that context. By the time you're home ‚Äî a different physical and cognitive context ‚Äî that intention has weakened or disappeared entirely.

Location-based reminders solve this by encoding and triggering the intention in the same context where the action will occur: your home.
